    Result of Declared Incident, Improved Property, Natural Features

    Appeal Brief Appeal Letter Appeal Analysis

    Appeal Brief

    Disaster4663
    ApplicantClay (County)
    Appeal TypeSecond
    PA ID#051-99051-00
    PW ID#GMP 693380/PW 994
    Date Signed2024-11-12T17:00:00

    Summary Paragraph

    During the incident period of July 26, 2022, to August 11, 2022, Kentucky was impacted by severe storms, flooding, and landslides. Clay (County) (Applicant) requested $184,494.93 in Public Assistance (PA) funding to repair multiple sites and implement hazard mitigation (HM) measures on Crane Branch Road. FEMA conducted a site inspection and took photographs of the claimed damages, which included alligator cracking, edge damages, potholes, and slope erosion. FEMA issued a request for information seeking documentation to support disaster caused damages. The Applicant responded by submitting a work log for Crane Branch Road, which did not include the specific location of the work performed. In a Determination Memorandum, FEMA denied $82,168.53 in costs to repair sites 13, 16, 17, 20, and 21 (Facility). FEMA found that the documentation submitted was insufficient to demonstrate the claimed damages were the direct result of the incident. The Applicant appealed, submitting new post-disaster photographs, and stating that its annual budget provides for the ongoing maintenance of the roads under its jurisdiction. The Kentucky Emergency Management requested FEMA reinstate all eligible components of the project. The FEMA Region 4 Regional Administrator denied the appeal, finding that the Applicant did not provide sufficient documentation demonstrating that the claimed damages occurred as a direct result of the disaster and, as result, any associated damage to the embankments was ineligible for PA. The Applicant submitted a second appeal reiterating previously raised arguments.

    Authorities

    • Stafford Act § 406(a)(1)(A).
    • 44 C.F.R. § 206.201(c), 206.223(a)(1), 206.226(e). 
    • PAPPG, at 51-52, 63-64, 153, 169, and 181-182.
    • Cumberland Cnty., FEMA-4595-DR-KY, at 2-3; Wayne (Cnty.), FEMA-4262-DR-MS, at 4; Marshall County, FEMA-4630-DR-KY, at 3; City of Fort Smith, FEMA-4441-DR-AR, at 5. 

    Headnotes

    • To be eligible, work must be required as a result of the declared incident.
      • The Applicant has not demonstrated that the claimed road damage was a result of the disaster.
    • A natural feature may itself be an eligible facility if it is improved and maintained. Alternatively, FEMA may approve PA funding to restore the integral ground that supports an eligible facility damaged by the disaster.
      • The Applicant has not demonstrated that the embankments have a designed and constructed improvement to its natural characteristics. The Applicant has also not demonstrated damage to an eligible facility, in this case the road, occurred as a result of the disaster. Thus, the embankment repairs are not eligible as integral ground.

    Conclusion

    FEMA finds that the Applicant has not demonstrated the claimed damages were the direct result of the declared incident. Consequently, work to repair the claimed damages and implement HM is ineligible. Therefore, this appeal is denied.

    Appeal Analysis

    Background

    During the incident period of July 26 through August 11, 2022, severe storms, flooding, landslides, and mudslides impacted the state of Kentucky.[1] Relevant to this appeal, Clay County (Applicant) stated that high flood water from the disaster damaged the surface, ditches, and embankments at sites 13, 16, 17, 20, and 21 of the Crane Branch Road, an asphalt road (Facility). The Applicant requested $82,168.53 in Public Assistance (PA) funding for repair and hazard mitigation (HM) measures. Specifically, the Applicant requested funding to address erosion of the Facility and adjacent embankments at sites 13, 16, 17, and 20, and erosion of the embankment at site 21.[2] On March 17, 2023, FEMA conducted a site inspection, photographed the claimed damages, and prepared a Site Inspection Report (SIR) describing the locations and the damage at each site on appeal. The SIR photographs showed alligator cracking, edge damage, and slope erosion. On June 21, 2023, FEMA issued a Request for Information seeking documentation to support that the disaster caused the claimed damages, including predisaster photographs, predisaster site specific invoices, and predisaster maintenance records. The Applicant responded by submitting a work log for the Facility, which included: the dates of predisaster work and a general description of the work performed to the Facility (e.g., “put barriers and a channel” and “repairing sides of the roads with channel liner”) without specifying specific sites; undated photographs; and 2021 invoices of gravel.[3] 

    In a Determination Memorandum (DM) issued on September 25, 2023, FEMA denied $82,168.53 in requested repair and HM costs. FEMA noted that the Applicant did not provide predisaster photographs, maintenance records, material invoices, activity logs, documentation of a pavement evaluation program, or pavement management system for the sites at issue. FEMA, therefore, found that because the Applicant had not produced documentation establishing the predisaster condition of the impacted sites, it had not demonstrated the claimed damages were the direct result of the declared incident.

    First Appeal

    In a letter dated November 23, 2023, the Applicant appealed requesting FEMA approve $82,168.53 in repair and HM costs for the five sites at issue. First, concerning the previously requested predisaster documentation, the Applicant claimed that FEMA imposed unreasonable standards for a municipality with limited staff and resources. Second, the Applicant submitted new post-disaster photographs of the claimed damages and stated that its annual budget provided for ongoing maintenance of the roads under its jurisdiction. Additionally, the Applicant clarified the damages it was claiming to the embankments.[4] In a transmittal letter dated November 30, 2023, Kentucky Emergency Management (Recipient) requested that FEMA approve all eligible components of the project. 

    On May 29, 2024, the FEMA Region 4 Regional Administrator denied the appeal. FEMA found that the Applicant did not provide documentation demonstrating that the claimed damages occurred as a direct result of the disaster. FEMA stated that the post-disaster photographs of the Facility showed damage indicative of age-related wear and tear, such as oxidization, raveling, and edge and fatigue cracking. Moreover, FEMA stated that because the Applicant did not demonstrate the damages to the Facility were caused by the disaster, any associated claimed damages to the adjacent embankments were ineligible for PA. Additionally, FEMA stated that the Applicant did not claim or provide documentation demonstrating that the embankments were eligible facilities.

    Second Appeal

    In a letter dated July 26, 2024, the Applicant submitted a second appeal requesting $82,168.53, reiterating previous arguments, and stating that the documentation provided satisfies FEMA’s requests for PA approval.  Additionally, the Applicant submits new post-disaster photographs of sites 16, 17, and 20, indicating that they show temporary work the Applicant performed to stabilize the Facility. Finally, the Applicant indicates that FEMA approved funding for this Facility under a different project.[5] In a transmittal letter dated August 9, 2024, the Recipient asks FEMA to reinstate all eligible components of the project. 

     

    Discussion

    FEMA may provide funding to a local government for the repair of public facilities damaged by a major disaster.[6] A natural feature may also be an eligible facility if it is improved and maintained and meets certain conditions.[7] Additionally, if an eligible public facility is located on a slope and is damaged as a result of a landslide or slope instability triggered by the incident, FEMA may approve PA funding for the restoration of the integral ground that supports the facility.[8] FEMA may provide PA funding for cost-effective HM measures for facilities damaged by the incident.[9] To be eligible for PA funding, work must be required as a result of the disaster.[10] FEMA does not provide funding for repair of damage caused by deterioration or deferred maintenance.[11] The incident may cause minor damage to roads that result in damage similar to that which may occur over time from other causes, such as the age of the road, traffic flow, and frequent rain.[12] It is the applicant’s responsibility to demonstrate that claimed damage was caused directly by the incident, and where pre-existing damage exists, to distinguish that damage from the disaster-related damage.[13] When necessary to validate damage, the applicant may be required to provide predisaster photographs of the impacted site or facility, and/or documentation supporting the predisaster condition of the facility (e.g., maintenance records, inspection/safety reports).[14] If the applicant does not provide documentation to support its claim as eligible, FEMA cannot provide PA funding for the work.[15]

    The Applicant states that damages to the Facility’s surface and/or adjacent embankments were a result of the declared disaster. However, the SIR photographs show minimal damages including alligator cracking, edge damage, potholes, and slope erosion, which may be associated with long-term deterioration and pre-existing damages.[16]The post-disaster photographs provided by the Applicant similarly do not assist in demonstrating the existence of disaster-caused damages. Therefore, to assist in distinguishing pre-existing damages from the claimed damages, FEMA requested predisaster photographs, predisaster site specific invoices, and predisaster maintenance records that could support the predisaster condition of the impacted sites. The Applicant submitted a work log listing work performed on multiple roads, including the Facility. However, the work log includes only dates, road names where a task was performed, and a general description of each task performed, such as “ditched Crane Branch,” without any additional description.[17] It does not establish the predisaster condition of the impacted sites or the predisaster condition thereto. Additionally, the Applicant claimed that it allocated funds in its annual budget for road repairs and that it had submitted an invoice for gravel purchased for the site. However, the invoice lists only item numbers, without descriptions identifying the expenses. 

    Based on the above, the Applicant has not demonstrated that claimed damages to the Facility and/or adjacent embankments were caused directly by the incident. Additionally, as the Applicant has not established the Facility sites at issue were damaged as a result of a landslide or slope instability triggered by the incident, funding to restore the adjacent embankments are also not eligible as integral ground. The embankment at site 21, which was not associated with any claimed damage to the Facility, is also ineligible for funding because it is not an eligible facility (i.e., an improved and maintained natural feature). Lastly, as PA funding for HM is only available for disaster-damaged facilities, the requested costs for HM are also ineligible.[18] 

     

    Conclusion

    FEMA finds that the Applicant has not demonstrated the claimed damages were the direct result of the declared incident. Consequently, work to repair the claimed damages and implement HM measures are ineligible. Therefore, this appeal is denied.
