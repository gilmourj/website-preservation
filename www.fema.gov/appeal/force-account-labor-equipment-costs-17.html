    Force Account Labor & Equipment Costs

    Appeal Brief Appeal Letter Appeal Analysis

    Appeal Brief

    Disaster4497
    ApplicantBaptist Health Madisonville, Inc.
    Appeal TypeSecond
    PA ID#107-UU251-00
    PW ID#GMP 683015/ PW 101
    Date Signed2024-11-12T17:00:00

    Summary Paragraph

    The COVID-19 pandemic resulted in a major disaster declaration on March 28, 2020, for the State of Kentucky. The Applicant, a Private Nonprofit (PNP) organization, requested reimbursement under FEMA’s Public Assistance (PA) program for emergency protective measures totaling $390,659.20 including costs for FAL straight-time and fringe benefits for employees to screen hospital visitors from September 11, 2021, to July 2, 2022. FEMA sent a Request for Information seeking documentation to clarify the temporary nature of the screening positions. The Applicant responded, including a letter describing the staff’s duties, further explaining the work, and categorizing the positions as temporary and unbudgeted. FEMA issued a Determination Memorandum denying $262,974.64 of the requested costs, including costs related to FAL straight-time for screener employees, increased operating costs, and administrative services. FEMA categorized all FAL costs requested as budgeted and stated that, for emergency work, only overtime labor was eligible for budgeted employees who performed screening and temperature scanning activities. The Applicant appealed, seeking only $246,235.10 in denied costs, contending that the screeners were temporary unbudgeted employees. The Applicant provided supporting documentation, including a screener status change notice; employee handbook; a spreadsheet and screeners’ time records; budget summaries; and a scoping memorandum. On June 7, 2024, the FEMA Region 4 Regional Administrator denied the appeal. FEMA found that the Applicant had not provided sufficient documentation demonstrating that the FAL straight-time costs and fringe benefits were directly related to performance of emergency protective measures eligible for PA reimbursement. The Applicant submitted its second appeal, reiterating its first appeal arguments. 

    Authorities

    • Stafford Act § 403(a)(3).
    • 44 C.F.R. § 206.225(a), 206.228(a).
    • PAPPG, at 23, 24, and 133.
    • O&O Policy, at 5.
    • Northwell Health, Inc., FEMA-4480-DR-NY, at 3; Mecklenburg County, FEMA-4487-DR-NC, at 3; South Kortright Central School FEMA-4480-DR-NY, at 4.

    Headnotes

    • For emergency work, except in limited circumstances, the straight-time of an applicant’s budgeted employees performing emergency work is not eligible. For unbudgeted employees performing emergency work, both straight-time and overtime labor are eligible.
      • The Applicant has not demonstrated its FAL straight-time and fringe benefit costs for emergency work were associated with unbudgeted screener employees. Accordingly, the FAL straight-time and fringe benefits’ costs for the scanner positions are not eligible for PA funding.

    Conclusion

    FEMA finds the Applicant has not demonstrated its FAL straight‑time and fringe benefits’ costs for emergency work were associated with eligible unbudgeted screener employees. Therefore, the appeal is denied.

    Appeal Analysis

    Background

    The (coronavirus) COVID-19 pandemic resulted in a major disaster on March 28, 2020, for the state of Kentucky, with an incident period of January 20, 2020, through May 11, 2023. Relevant to this appeal, Baptist Health Madisonville, Inc. (Applicant), a Private Nonprofit (PNP) organization, requested Public Assistance (PA) reimbursement totaling $390,659.20 for emergency protective measures it performed from January 20, 2020, to June 30, 2022. The request included costs for force account labor (FAL) straight-time and fringe benefits for employees to screen hospital visitors from September 11, 2021, to July 2, 2022.[1] FEMA prepared Grants Manager Project 683015 to document the costs associated with this work.

    The Applicant provided a position conversion notification, dated September 15, 2020, showing it transitioned temporary screening associate employees to permanent full-time employees, effective September 13, 2020.[2] The notification states these positions would be maintained “for an undetermined time” and the employee’s new “permanent” status would qualify for employee benefits, including paid time off and health insurance. The Applicant stated it transitioned employees to permanent positions, with benefits, to reduce turnover and increase retention. FEMA sent a Request for Information seeking documentation to clarify the temporary nature of the screening positions. The Applicant responded, including a letter describing the staff’s duties (taking individual’s temperatures and ensuring masks were worn), further explaining the work, and categorizing the positions and employees as temporary contractors and unbudgeted.

    On June 15, 2023, FEMA issued a Determination Memorandum, denying $262,974.64 of the requested costs, including costs related to FAL straight-time and fringe benefits of employees who performed screening and temperature scanning activities.[3] FEMA categorized all FAL costs requested as budgeted (i.e., permanent) and stated that, for emergency work, only overtime labor was eligible for budgeted employees who performed screening and temperature scanning activities.

    First Appeal

    On August 14, 2023, the Applicant submitted its first appeal, seeking $246,235.10 in previously denied costs that it stated were associated with FAL straight-time and fringe benefits for temporary employees hired for screening and temperature scanning.[4] The Applicant stated that: 1) the screener positions were established as temporary positions; 2) the positions were transitioned to permanent full-time to retain employees on a temporary basis; 3) the positions were eliminated before the end of the incident period; and 4) it no longer employed the temperature screeners. The Applicant contended that the conversion letter stated the positions would remain for an undetermined amount of time because the positions would be removed as the COVID-19 pandemic and COVID-19 patient cases decreased over time. The Applicant reiterated that all the screener positions were eliminated before the end of the COVID-19 pandemic and none of the screener employees were currently employed by the Applicant.

    The Applicant provided its employee handbook that contains “compensation guidelines,” which generally describe its pay policy and explain temporary full-time and part-time employees’ classifications and benefits based on the length of the employee’s employment and the number of hours worked;[5] a scoping memorandum explaining permanent and temporary staff duties and the nature of unbudgeted positions, including entry screeners, whose listed duties were to ask COVID-19 related health questions, take temperatures, and provide masks and entry stickers to all hospital entrants; screener position job codes; a spreadsheet listing some of the claimed employees associated with some job codes and screeners’ clocking records; and budget summaries for 2019 through 2022 showing allocated expenses for salaries and contract labor. On August 5, 2023, Kentucky Emergency Management (Recipient) transmitted the appeal to FEMA with its support.

    On June 7, 2024, the FEMA Region 4 Regional Administrator denied the appeal. FEMA found the Applicant had not provided sufficient documentation demonstrating the FAL straight-time and fringe benefits’ costs were eligible for PA funding. Specifically, FEMA stated the Applicant did not provide: 1) work agreements for each of the claimed temporary employees; 2) documentation demonstrating that each of the claimed employees was only hired or reassigned to perform eligible work (temperature screening); 3) timesheets for each of the claimed employees; 4) a spreadsheet clearly identifying the “who, what, when, where, why, and how much” for each claimed employee clearly linking the claimed cost for each employee to eligible temperature screener work; and 5) an appeal amount matching the total FAL claimed on an itemized spreadsheet.

    Second Appeal

    On August 1, 2024, the Applicant submitted its second appeal, seeking $246,235.10 in denied costs and reiterating its first appeal arguments. The Applicant emphasizes its emergency response to COVID-19 was consistent with its legal responsibilities to protect public health and safety and asserts its costs were reasonable in accordance with FEMA policy.[6] On August 9, 2024, the Recipient transmitted the second appeal to FEMA with its support.

     

    Discussion

    FEMA is authorized to provide assistance for emergency protective measures to save lives and to protect public health and safety.[7]In response to COVID-19, eligible emergency protective measures include screening and temperature scanning to facilitate the safe opening and operation of all eligible facilities.[8] FEMA reimburses FAL based on actual hourly rates plus the cost of the employee’s actual fringe benefits.[9] For emergency work, except in limited circumstances, the FAL straight-time of an applicant’s budgeted employees performing emergency work is not eligible.[10] For unbudgeted employees performing emergency work, both FAL straight-time and FAL overtime labor are eligible.[11] An applicant’s permanent employees are considered to be budgeted employees, while temporary employees hired to perform eligible work are unbudgeted employees.[12] FEMA requires documentation to support that the work is eligible, which should include the “who, what, when, where, why, and how much” for each claimed item.[13] It is the applicant’s responsibility to provide documentation to substantiate its claim as eligible and to clearly explain how those records support its appeal.[14] 

    Here, the Applicant requests reimbursement for FAL straight-time and associated fringe benefits’ costs it incurred by paying employees who it contends were temporary unbudgeted employees performing emergency protective measures of screening and temperature scanning those entering the hospital to prevent and protect its patients, visitors, and staff from COVID-19 infection or exposure. However, the Applicant provided notification to all screening associates converting them to permanent employee status effective September 13, 2020 (before the 2021 to 2022 timeframe during which the current costs at issue were incurred). Thus, the Applicant’s own documentation defines these scanner positions as permanent positions, eligible for employee benefits. Additionally, based on the notification effective date, all the FAL costs at issue in this appeal were associated with permanent employees. Consequently, based on the above, the Applicant has not demonstrated the FAL straight-time and fringe benefits’ costs for the screener positions are associated with unbudgeted employees. Therefore, the requested costs are not eligible for PA funding.

     

    Conclusion

    The Applicant has not demonstrated its FAL straight-time and fringe benefits’ costs for emergency work were associated with eligible unbudgeted screener employees. Therefore, the appeal is denied.
